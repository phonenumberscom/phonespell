The Letter Map

What letters go with each number?

On every modern telephone keypad, the digits 2 through 9 each carry a group of letters: 2 = ABC, 3 = DEF, 4 = GHI, 5 = JKL, 6 = MNO, 7 = PQRS, 8 = TUV, 9 = WXYZ. The digits 1 and 0 carry no letters at all.

Reading a number as letters

To convert phone number letters in either direction, just follow the map: each letter is dialed as its key's digit, so FLOWERS becomes 356-9377 and PLUMBER becomes 758-6237. Going the other way is trickier — each digit could be any of its three or four letters, which is why finding the words hidden in a number is a job for our phone number to letters converter.

Where Q and Z live

Q sits on the 7 key and Z on the 9 key. Neither letter existed on old rotary dials — they were skipped so each hole could hold exactly three letters. The modern ITU E.161 standard added them back so the whole alphabet is dialable.

Where It Came From

Why phones have letters at all

The letters predate the keypad itself — they were how people dialed before all-number calling.

1920s — exchange names

Numbers were dialed as words: "PEnnsylvania 6-5000" meant dialing P-E, then 6-5000. Letters on the rotary dial made whole telephone exchanges memorable.

1963 — the touch-tone grid

Bell's push-button phones kept the letters in the now-familiar 3×4 grid. The ITU later standardized the map worldwide — adding Q to 7 and Z to 9, which old dials had skipped.

1990s — T9 and texting

The same letter groups powered T9 predictive text — tap 4-3-5-5-6 and the phone guessed "hello." Today the map lives on in vanity numbers and phone menus.

In the exchange-name era, a phone number was written with its first letters capitalized — PEnnsylvania 6-5000, KLondike 5, MUrray Hill 7 — and the capitals told you which letters to dial. The format even made it into pop culture: Glenn Miller's "PEnnsylvania 6-5000" is literally a phone number, and Hollywood's fictional "KLondike 5" numbers are why movie characters still recite numbers starting with 555.

When direct long-distance dialing arrived, exchange names couldn't scale — too many name collisions across cities — so the Bell System phased in all-number calling through the 1960s. The letters could have died there. Instead, businesses realized the map turned a phone number into a word you could advertise, and the phoneword was born: first on radio jingles, then on billboards, trucks, and 1-800 lines that became brands in their own right.

The Missing Keys

Why 0 and 1 have no letters

On early rotary systems, 1 was reserved for signaling and long-distance prefixes and 0 rang the operator — so neither digit was ever assigned letters. That's why every phoneword is built only from digits 2 through 9, and why 0s and 1s pass through a vanity number unchanged (the 1-800 in 1-800-FLOWERS is always dialed as digits).

FAQ

Keypad letter questions, answered

What letters correspond to each number on a phone?
2 = ABC, 3 = DEF, 4 = GHI, 5 = JKL, 6 = MNO, 7 = PQRS, 8 = TUV, and 9 = WXYZ. The digits 1 and 0 have no letters.
Why do phone keypads have letters?
Letters were added in the 1920s so callers could dial memorable exchange names like "PEnnsylvania 6-5000" instead of raw digits. The system survived into touch-tone phones and now powers vanity numbers, T9-style texting, and dial-by-name directories.
How do I dial a phone number with letters in it?
Press the digit that carries each letter: 1-800-FLOWERS is dialed 1-800-356-9377. The letters are only a memory aid — the phone network only ever sees digits, so any keypad can dial any phoneword.
Where are Q and Z on the phone keypad?
Q is on the 7 key and Z is on the 9 key. Old rotary dials skipped both letters; the modern ITU E.161 standard added them so every letter of the alphabet can be dialed.
Which numbers have no letters?
1 and 0. On early phone systems 1 was reserved for signaling and long-distance prefixes and 0 rang the operator, so neither was ever assigned letters — they always stay as digits in a phoneword.
Do all phones use the same keypad letters?
Yes — the ITU E.161 standard puts the same letters on every modern device, from smartphones to desk phones to door-entry keypads. Only antique dials differ: many skipped Q and Z, and some European rotary phones carried no letters at all.
How do I type letters as a phone number?
